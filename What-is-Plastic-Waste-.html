What is Plastic Waste?

Plastic waste refers to plastic materials that are discarded after use and are not properly managed, recycled, or disposed of. Unlike natural materials, plastic does not easily break down in the environment. Instead, it can remain in landfills, rivers, and oceans for hundreds of years.



Plastic waste is not just trash. It's a threat to our ecosystem!

How do Plastic Reach the Sea?

Because plastic is lightweight and durable, it travels easily from land to sea through rivers, drainage systems, and ocean currents. Once in the ocean, it breaks down into smaller pieces called microplastics, which are extremely difficult to remove. It is a long-term threat to marine ecosystems and fishing livelihoods.

Land-Based Sources

Land → River → Coast → Ocean

Most marine plastic actually comes from land. When garbage is not collected properly, it can be carried by rainwater into rivers, streams, and drainage systems that lead to the sea. During heavy rains and typhoons, massive amounts of plastic waste are swept into coastal waters.


Common land-based sources include:


  • Improperly disposed household trash
  • Litter from markets and streets
  • Plastic from poorly managed dumpsites
  • Waste from rivers and estuaries

Sea-Based Sources

Plastic also enters the ocean directly from human activities at sea. Fishermen, shipping vessels, and tourism boats sometimes leave behind plastic waste, either accidentally or intentionally.


Sea-based sources include:


  • Abandoned or lost fishing nets (ghost nets)
  • Plastic bottles and packaging from boats
  • Damaged fishing gear left in the water
  • Litter from beach visitors and tourists
